# Bullwhip Effect: Game Concept

## One-Paragraph Concept

You are one link in a chain. You see only your own inventory, the orders you receive from downstream, and the delays before your own orders arrive from upstream. You must place orders now for goods that will arrive later, while the number you see—“demand”—is already distorted by everyone else’s past decisions. The game never shows you the full system, the true end demand, or the future. Your job is to keep your station from running out or drowning in stock. Small overreactions—ordering a bit more because last week was scary—ripple backward and forward in time: your suppliers overreact to you, you overreact to noisy signals, and the whole chain oscillates. Success feels like dull, stable flow; failure feels like chaos, stockouts, and the creeping regret that your own “fixes” made things worse. You learn the bullwhip by feeling it: the pressure of incomplete information, the temptation to panic-order, and the cost of seeking short-term comfort instead of long-term stability.

---

## Step-by-Step Gameplay Loop

1. **See your world (partial)**  
   You see: your current inventory, incoming orders (what’s already in the pipeline), and the “orders you received” from downstream this period. You do **not** see: true end-customer demand, other players’ inventories, or future demand. Numbers are delayed and lumpy.

2. **Commit, don’t tweak**  
   You choose one thing: **order quantity** (or equivalent commitment) for the next window. That decision is **locked** for a fixed number of turns (e.g. 2–3). You cannot take it back. The game does not let you optimize every single period.

3. **Wait (delayed feedback)**  
   Time advances. Your order travels upstream (lead time). Other links’ orders and behavior affect what actually arrives. You see outcomes only when they happen: late deliveries, surprises, inventory piling up or collapsing.

4. **Experience the wave**  
   Your past commitments land. So do everyone else’s. You see your inventory and maybe a simple “stress” or “disruption” signal—not a full dashboard. You feel surplus or shortage, and perhaps a hint that “upstream is struggling” or “downstream is panicking” without exact numbers.

5. **React under pressure**  
   You make the next commitment with the same limited view. The temptation is to “fix” the last shock (order more, order less). Those reactions are exactly what the system amplifies. The game does not explain this; the loop makes it visible over time.

6. **Reflect (after the wave)**  
   At chosen moments (e.g. after a round or a crisis), the game asks not “what should you have ordered?” but “what did you feel when you saw that spike?” and “what did you do next?” Cause and effect are revealed in hindsight: your decisions, delays, and others’ reactions combine into the bullwhip. The player infers forecast error, lead time distortion, and amplification from their own experience.

7. **Loop**  
   Repeat with the same or slightly changed rules (e.g. longer lead time, noisier demand). Tension comes from commitment, delay, and the sense that the system is bigger and messier than what you see.

---

## Core Tension (1–2 Sentences)

You must act with incomplete, delayed, and distorted information, and every time you try to “fix” a problem you often feed the next one—yet doing nothing feels reckless. The fun and the learning live in that squeeze: trusting the system enough to stay stable without having the information to fully justify that trust.

---

## What Makes This Fun Despite Being Educational

- **Stakes without a spreadsheet:** The tension is “will I run out?” and “did I just make everything worse?” not “did I hit the optimal EOQ?”  
- **Stories, not metrics:** Players remember “that time I panicked and the whole chain went crazy” and “that run where I held my nerve and it actually calmed down.”  
- **Aha in hindsight:** The teaching moment is the moment you see that your own “sensible” reaction was the trigger—learning is discovery, not instruction.  
- **Replayability:** Different lead times, noise levels, and commitment windows make each run feel different; mastering your own impulses is the real skill.  
- **Shared vulnerability:** If multi-player or class-based, everyone is in the same uncertain system; blame and credit are fuzzy, which mirrors real supply chains.

---

## Design Benchmark

If the player says: **“I felt the bullwhip effect before I could explain it,”** the concept is successful.
